Microscopes

Revision Notes

Since cells are incredibly small (most cells are only a few micrometers, one ten-thousandth of a centimetre, large), we need to use a microscope in order to observe them properly.

A microscope has lots of complex parts and as such you must learn what they are and what they do:

The eyepiece lens(es) is the part you look into and has a small magnification (usually 10x) in order to help you see the cells better.

However, most of the magnification comes from the objective lenses, of which there are usually three. The magnifications of these are usually any three of 4x, 10x, 40x and 100x, and they can be interchanged when observing a cell. We use the smallest one first because it gives us a greater depth of field, and as such we can locate the cell before zooming into it.

The stage is where the cells are placed for viewing with a slide. A light sits underneath to light up the cells and make them clearer. The position of the stage can be controlled with a knob.

Lastly, the focus controls, of which there is a coarse focus and a fine focus. They both move the lens closer to the cells, but the coarse one moves faster. This allows you to quickly focus in with the coarse one and then obtain the exact desired focus with the fine one.

Equations

We can calculate the total magnification of the microscope with the equation:

total magnification = eyepiece magnification x objective magnification

There is also an equation relating to the real size, magnification and image size of a specimen under a microscope. We can use two of these to find the third one, i.e. using the image size and magnification to find the real size of a cell. The equations are as follows:

total magnification = image size / actual size

real size = image size / total magnification

image size = real size x total magnification

Electron microscopes

In 1933, scientists discovered a new way to look at cells in even more detail - the electron microscope.

This microscope fires electrons at the cell and then uses a camera to build up an image of the cell in incredible detail.

Images taken with an electron microscope can have a much higher magnification and resolution (the amount of detail visible) and so they allow us to see very small things that we previously were unable to.

The disadvantages of an electron microscope are that it cannot take colour images (as it is not using light) and it cannot be used to scan living cells (because it creates a vacuum when it takes images). They are also incredibly expensive and usually only found in universities and laboratories.

Quick Quiz


Practice and Exam Questions

Note that the Practice Question(s) do not confine to the exam spec exactly but rather test knowledge on the subject.

  1. An onion cell is shown in a textbook with a width of 2cm. Given that the onion cell’s real size is 0.25mm, what is the magnification of the image?


    Show Answer

    The magnification is 80x.

  2. Name two advantages of an electron microscope compared to a standard light microscope. You should aim to make at least two points.


    Show Answer

    Any two of:

    • it gives a higher resolution

    • it can give a higher magnification

    • it can see tiny cells and organisms that a light microscope cannot

  3. A cell of width 340μm is magnified by 100 times. What is the image size, in cm?


    Show Answer

    The image size is 3.4cm.

  4. When observing a slide under a microscope, first the ____ focus must be used.


    Show Answer

    The missing word is 'coarse'.

  5. Why is the smallest objective lens used first when observing something with a microscope?


    Show Answer

    It allows for a greater depth of field, making it easier to find things before zooming in.
