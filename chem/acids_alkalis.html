Acids and Alkalis

Revision Notes

Most substances are either alkaline, basic or acidic, a quality based on the hydrogen in the substance.

The pH Scale

By Christinelmiller - Own work, CC BY-SA 4.0

The pH (potential of hydrogen) scale tells us how acidic or basic/alkaline a substance is. Something with a high concentration of hydrogen ions (H+) is acidic, while something with a low concentration of hydrogen ions is basic/alkaline. We can use the pH scale to represent these numerically, from 1 being the most acidic to 14 being the most basic/alkaline. In theory the scale goes infinitely in both directions, and you can have a negative pH, but getting substances that are so strongly acidic or basic is difficult and so at GCSE, we only need to know about 1 to 14.

pH IS A LOGARITHMIC SCALE!

This means that for every increase/decrease of 1 pH, there is a x10 increase/decrease of hydrogen concentration.

So pH 1 is x1000 more concentrated with hydrogen ions than pH 4.

A substance with a pH of 1 has 0.1 mole/dm3 (a measurement of concentration) of hydrogen, pH 2 is 0.01 mole/dm3, etc.

Substances with a pH below 7 are acidic, and substances above 7 are basic. The further from 7, the stronger the acid/base. Substances with a pH of 7 are neutral, and are neither acidic nor basic.

Bases and Alkalis

A base is any substance with a pH of 8 or higher, and is also defined as a substance which accepts hydrogen ions, in a solution.

An alkali is a soluble base, i.e. it can be dissolved in water.

Alkalis release hydroxide ions (OH-) when dissolved.

The following compounds are ALWAYS bases:

  • metal oxides (metal and oxygen)
  • metal carbonates (metal, carbon and oxygen)

Equally, the following compounds are always alkalis, and so are also bases:

  • ammonia (NH3)
  • metal hydroxides

Acids

Acids are substances with a pH of 6 or lower, and are also defined as a donor of hydrogen ions in a solution.

Unlike bases, acids never contain metals and instead usually contain hydrogen and sometimes oxygen with other non-metals.

Acids release hydrogen ions (H+) when dissolved.

These acids are absolutely vital, and you need to know their name and formula:

  • sulfuric acid - H2SO4
  • hydrochloric acid - HCl
  • nitric acid - HNO3

Another one you'll need to know at GCSE is phosphoric acid (H3PO4, although it is less important than the three above.

Metals and Water

We can react metals with water (H2O). On our standard reactivity series, this only applies to potassium (K), sodium (Na), and calcium (Ca); the other metals on our series are not reactive enough to react with water directly.

This reaction forms metal hydroxides, with the following equation:

metal + water -> metal hydroxide + hydrogen

For example, reacting sodium with water gives us sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and hydrogen gas.

Reactions with water are often quite vigorous, and can sometimes generate enough heat to ignite the hydrogen gas which is produced - potassium, for example, produces a lilac flame when in water.

If we add universal indicator, it will turn blue. This tells us that the metal hydroxide is alkaline.

Neutralisation

Acids and alkalis can be reacted together which neutralises them to form a salt. This is called a neutralisation reaction and more information about these reactions can be found here.

Quick Quiz

Practice and Exam Questions

Note that the Practice Question(s) do not confine to the exam spec exactly but rather test knowledge on the subject.

  1. A reaction takes place: potassium + x -> potassium hydroxide.
    1. What is the substance x?
      Show Answer

      Substance x is water, as metals react with water to form metal hydroxides.

    2. The same reaction is attempted but with raw copper instead of potassium. A reaction does not take place and copper hydroxide is not produced. Why?
      Show Answer

      Raw copper is not reactive enough to react with water.

  2. An acid has a pH of 1. Another acid has a concentration of hydrogen of 0.0001 mol/dm3. What is the pH of this acid?
    Show Answer

    pH is a logarithmic scale, and so as the pH number increases the concentration is divided by 10. pH 1 = 0.1 mol/dm3, and so to get 0.0001 mol/dm3 you must divide by 10 three times. 1 + 3 = 4, so the pH of the acid is 4.

  3. Tim says that iron oxide is soluble in water. Is Tim correct? Explain your answer.
    Show Answer

    Tim is incorrect. Metal oxides are bases, and so they do not dissolve in water.
