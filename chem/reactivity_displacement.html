Reactivity and Displacement

Revision Notes

Different metals on the periodic table have different reactivities. The reactivity of a metal determines how easily and vigorously it will react with other substances, and we usually look at reactions with acids and water when comparing reactivity.

Displacement

Metals have some unusual reactions. For example, if we react copper oxide with iron:

copper oxide + iron -> iron oxide + copper

The compounds have swapped! What if we did it the other way around?

iron oxide + copper -> ... (no reaction)

There's no reaction at all! So what has happened?

We can describe this behaviour as displacement. In short, metals can displace other metals from compounds (remove them and take their place), but only if the metal is more reactive than the one in the compound. Iron is more reactive than copper, and so it was able to displace copper from its oxide compound.

There are specific terms we can use to refer to what happens to each element. In this case, iron has gained oxygen (it has become an oxide), and so we can say that it has been oxidised. The copper, by contrast, has lost oxygen (it has been removed from its oxide), and so we can say that it has been reduced. This reaction is called a redox reaction, as both reduction and oxidation have taken place. We can also call reduction the gaining of electrons and oxidation the losing of electrons, although this relates to ions and is beyond the scope of this topic.

No mass is lost or gained in displacement reactions. If a gas is produced then it may appear that mass is lost, but this lost mass is actually the gas.

Carbon Reduction and Extraction

You might notice that our reactivity series diagram has carbon on it. This isn't because carbon reacts in the same way as metals with acids and water - it doesn't. We often put carbon on the series so that we can compare it with metals, and this is important for carbon reduction.

In order for metals to be useful, we have to extract them from their ores (their compounds such as oxides). There are a variety of ways to do this, but for metals which are less reactive than carbon, we can displace the metal with carbon. This is done at high temperatures in a blast furnace. For example, we can extract iron from its ore (iron oxide) as follows:

iron oxide + carbon -> carbon dioxide + iron

As you can see, the carbon has removed the iron from its oxide, leaving carbon dioxide (which can be vented from the furnace) and pure iron.

However, metals that are more reactive than carbon cannot be extracted using it. As a result, we must extract these differently, either with a more reactive metal or via electrolysis.

Finally, gold, copper and silver are so unreactive that they often do not react with oxygen, and can be found in a raw metal state. In this case, extraction is not required at all.

  1. There are 4 metals, Metal A, Metal B, Metal C and Metal D.
    Metal A + Metal B oxide -> Metal A oxide + Metal B.
    Metal C + Metal D oxide -> ... (no reaction).
    Metal A + Metal D oxide -> Metal A oxide + Metal D.
    Metal B + Metal C oxide -> ... (no reaction).

    1. Which metal is the most reactive?

      If we fill in a table with the metals on one side and their oxides on the other based on whether a displacement takes place, we can make some logical judgements about which metal is the most reactive. Once the table is filled in we can see that Metal A can displace all of the other metals, and as such it is the most reactive.

    2. Which is more reactive - Metal B or Metal D?

      Show Answer

      From the table, we can see that Metal B does not displace any other metal, while Metal D can displace both Metal B and Metal C. As such, Metal D is more reactive.

  2. An unknown metal oxide is reacted with carbon. They are placed in a test tube, which is sealed. A test tube of limewater connected to the test tube turns cloudy, indicating the presence of carbon dioxide.
    Tom, a student who observed the experiment, says that the metal cannot be zinc. Is Tom correct? Explain your answer.

    Show Answer

    Tom is not correct, as zinc is less reactive than carbon and so can be displaced from its oxide by carbon, resulting in zinc and carbon dioxide.
