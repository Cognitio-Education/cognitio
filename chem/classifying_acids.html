Classifying Acids

Revision Notes

We know already that an acid is a donor of a H+ ion to a solution. But are all acids the same?
As it turns out, there are significant differences between acids, and so we classify them into two types:

  • STRONG ACIDS - H+ fully ionises
  • WEAK ACIDS - H+ partially ionises

This chapter will explain what this means in more detail.

Strong Acids

Strong acids are the less erratic of the two, as the hydrogen will ionise fully.

This means that when we dissolve the acid in water, we always get one molecule of H+ for every molecule of acid, as the acid is always dissociated, meaning that it splits into its ions.

For example, hydrochloric acid is strong.

hydrochloric acid + water (dissolving) -> dissolved hydrogen + dissolved chlorine

HCl (g) + (aq) -> H+ (aq) + Cl- (aq)

Remember that (g) means "gas", and (aq) means aqueous (i.e. dissolved in water).

As you can see, we directly get one H+ ion for each acid molecule.

The three main acids: sulfuric, hydrochloric and nitric are all strong and act in this way. For instance, nitric acid:

HNO3 (g) + (aq) -> H+ (aq) + NO3- (aq)

Sulfuric acid acts in a more unusual manner and so we do not look at its formula at GCSE.

Weak Acids

Weak acids, however, do not dissociate fully, and so a hydrogen ion does not always form - usually at a chanvce of about 1 in 10,000 does an ion actually form. This makes the dissociation of weak acids reversible, because it is weakly associated. For example, methanoic acid:

HCOOH (l) + (aq) <-> H+ (aq) + HCOO- (aq)*

*: at a chance of about 1 in 10,000

More often than not, the molecules dissolved in the water do not split and stay together as an acid.
